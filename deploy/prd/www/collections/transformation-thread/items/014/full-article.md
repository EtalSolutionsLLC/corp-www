## The Fix Is Usually Smaller Than the Problem

Every week I see the same pattern: a team wrestling with some recurring operational drag, convinced they need a new system, a new workflow, or some big “modernization” push. But when you actually sit with the problem, the fix is almost always tiny.

Not glamorous. Not technical. Just tiny.

And it’s wild how often that tiny fix does more than any tool they were about to buy.

### The Real Drag Isn’t the Work — It’s the Workaround

People are incredibly good at adapting. They build little side rituals to survive bad processes:

- a spreadsheet someone updates at 7am  
- a Slack message that’s basically a second inbox  
- a mental checklist only one person remembers  

None of these look like “problems.” They look like normal work. But they quietly eat hours and attention.

And because they’re invisible, nobody questions them.

### The Question That Cuts Through All the Noise

Before you reach for a tool, ask:

> **“What’s the smallest structural change that would make this stop happening?”**

Not the biggest fix.  
Not the most impressive fix.  
Just the smallest one that kills the recurrence.

It’s almost always something like:

- one clearer definition  
- one decision rule  
- one field added to a form  
- one handoff tightened  

And *then* maybe a bit of A.I. or automation to reinforce it — but only after the foundation is clear.

### A.I. Works Best When You Already Know What You’re Doing

Your newsroom piece says it well: A.I. needs adult supervision.

It’s great at amplifying clarity.  
It’s terrible at creating clarity.

If the underlying decision is fuzzy, A.I. just makes the fuzz faster.

### The Move for Today

If something keeps slowing you down, don’t start with tools.

Start with the operational drag.  
Name it plainly.  
Find the smallest structural fix.  
Then decide whether tech should support it.

That’s the whole game.  
Small moves, big impact.
